5.3 Laws of Large Numbers

Let \((X_{n})\) be a sequence of random variables all defined on the same probability space, that have the following properties,

  • • they are independent;

  • • they are identically distributed i.e. \(p_{X_{n}} = p_{X_{m}}\) for all \(n \neq m\). In other words, for all \(A \in {\cal B}(\R )\),

    \[ \P [X_{1} \in A] = \P [X_{2} \in A] = \cdots = \P [X_{n} \in A] = \cdots \]

Such a sequence is said to be ‘i.i.d.’. Sequences of this type are very important in modelling (consider the steps of a random walk) and also statistics (consider a sequence of idealised experiments carried out under identical conditions).

We are interested in the empirical mean

\[ \overline {X_{n}} = \frac {1}{n}(X_{1} + X_{2} + \cdots + X_{n}),\]

which in statistics is often known as the sample mean. If \(X_{n}\) is integrable for some (and hence all) \(\nN \), with \(\mu =\E [X_n]\), then by linearity \(\overline {X_{n}}\) is integrable and \(\E [\overline {X_{n}}] = \mu \). It is extremely important to learn about the asymptotic behaviour of \(\overline {X_{n}}\) as \(n \rightarrow \infty \). Two key results are the weak law of large numbers (WLLN) and the strong law of large numbers (SLLN). The strong law implies the weak law, but it is much trickier to prove. We will include a full proof of the weak law and a partial proof of the strong law.

  • Theorem 5.3.1 (WLLN) Let \((X_{n})\) be a sequence of integrable i.i.d. random variables with \(\E (X_{n}) = \mu \) for all \(\nN \). Suppose also that \(\E [X_{n}^{2}] < \infty \) for all \(\nN \). Then \(\overline {X_{n}} \rightarrow \mu \) in probability as \(n \rightarrow \infty \).

Proof: Let \(\sigma ^{2} = \var (X_{n})\) for all \(\nN \). Since the \((X_n)\) are independent, it follows that \(\var (\overline {X_n})=\frac {\sigma ^2}{n}\). Then by Chebychev’s inequality, for all \(a > 0\),

\(\seteqnumber{0}{5.}{5}\)

\begin{align*} \P \l [|\overline {X_{n}} - \mu | > a\r ] \leq \frac {\var (\overline {X_{n}})}{a^{2}} = \frac {\sigma ^{2}}{na^{2}} \end{align*} which tends to zero as \(n\to \infty \).   ∎

  • Theorem 5.3.2 (SLLN) Let \((X_{n})\) be a sequence of integrable i.i.d. random variables with \(\E (X_{n}) = \mu \) for all \(\nN \). Then \(\overline {X_{n}} \rightarrow \mu \) almost surely as \(n \rightarrow \infty \).

The full proof of the SLLN is too long for this course. You can find it in e.g. Rosenthal on pages 47-49. We’ll give the proof in a special case, by making an assumption on the fourth moments of the sequence \((X_{n})\), and then remark on the general case.

  • Assumption 5.3.3 \(\E [(X_{n} - \mu )^{4}] = b < \infty \) for all \(\nN \).

Proof:[Of Theorem 5.3.2 under Assumption 5.3.3.] Without loss of generality we may assume that \(\mu =0\). To see this, instead of \(X_{n}\) we would consider \(Y_{n} = X_{n}- \mu \).

Let \(S_{n} = X_{1} + X_{2} + \cdots + X_{n}\) so that \(S_{n} = n\overline {X_{n}}\) for all \(\nN \). Consider \(\E [S_{n}^{4}]\). It contains many terms of the form \(\E [X_{j}X_{k}X_{l}X_{m}]\) with distinct indices and these all vanish by independence, because \(\E [X_j]=\E [X_k]=\E [X_l]=\E [X_m]=0\). A similar argument disposes of terms of the form \(\E [X_{j}X_{k}^{3}]\) and \(\E [X_{j}X_{k}X_{l}^{2}]\), where \(j,k,l\) are distinct. The only terms with non-vanishing expectation are \(n\) terms of the form \(X_{i}^{4}\) and \({n \choose 2}{4 \choose 2} = 3n(n-1)\) terms of the form \(X_{i}^{2}X_{j}^{2}\) with \(i \neq j\). By Theorem 4.4.2, \(X_{i}^{2}\) and \(X_{j}^{2}\) are independent for \(i \neq j\) and so by Theorem 4.4.2

\[ \E [X_{i}^{2}X_{j}^{2}] = \E [X_{i}^{2}]E[X_{j}^{2}] = \var (X_{i}^{2})\var (X_{j}^{2}) = \sigma ^{4}.\]

Putting all this together,

\(\seteqnumber{0}{5.}{5}\)

\begin{align*} \E [S_{n}^{4}] & = \sum _{i=1}^{n}\E [X_{i}^{4}] + \sum _{i \neq j}\E [X_{i}^{2}X_{j}^{2}]\\ & = nb + 3n(n-1)\sigma ^{4} \leq K n^{2}, \end{align*} where \(K = b + 3\sigma ^{4}\). For all \(a > 0\), by Markov’s inequality (Lemma 3.3.1)

\(\seteqnumber{0}{5.}{5}\)

\begin{align*} \P [|\overline {X_{n}}| > a] = \P [S_{n}^{4} > a^{4}n^{4}] \leq \frac {\E [S_{n}^{4}]}{a^{4}n^{4}} \leq \ds \frac {Kn^{2}}{a^{4}n^{4}} = \ds \frac {K}{a^{4}n^{2}}. \end{align*} Recall that \(\sum _{n=1}^{\infty }\frac {1}{n^{2}} < \infty \). Hence, by the first Borel-Cantelli lemma, \(\P [|\overline {X_{n}}| > a\;\text {i.o.}] = 0\) and so \(\P [|\overline {X_{n}}| \leq a\;\text {e.v.}] = 1\). It follows that \(\overline {X_{n}} \rightarrow 0\) a.s. as required.   ∎

  • Remark 5.3.4 (\(\star \)) The proof, in the general case without Assumption 4.1, uses a truncation argument, based on \(Y_{n} = X_{n}{\1}_{\{X_{n} \leq n\}}\). Then \(Y_{n} \leq n\) for all \(n\) and so \(\E [Y_{n}^{k}] \leq n^{k}\) for all \(k\). If \(X_{n} \geq 0\) for all \(n\), \(\E [Y_{n}] \rightarrow \mu \) by monotone convergence. Roughly speaking the argument is to prove a SLLN for the \(\overline {Y_{n}}\)s, and then transfer this to the \(\overline {X_{n}}\)s. It requires much more work.

If \(\E (X_{n}^{2}) < \infty \) then (by Problem 4.9) \(\var (X_{n}) = \sigma ^{2} < \infty \) for some (and hence all) \(\nN \). It follows by elementary properties of the variance that \(\var (\overline {X_{n}}) = \frac {\sigma ^{2}}{n}\). This is known as the empirical variance or sample variance. The Central Limit Theorem investigates this quantity, as we will see in Section 5.5.
