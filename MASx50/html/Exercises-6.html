6.4 Exercises \((\Delta )\)

Throughout these problems \((S_{1}, \Sigma _{1}, m_{1})\) and \((S_{2}, \Sigma _{2}, m_{2})\) are measure spaces. From Problem 3 onwards they are always \(\sigma \)-finite.

  • 6.1 If \(E, F \subset S_{1} \times S_{2}\) and \(x \in S_{1}\), show that

    • (a) \((E \cap F)_{x} = E_{x} \cap F_{x}\),

    • (b) \((E^{c})_{x} = (E_{x})^{c}\),

    • (c) \(\left (\bigcup _{n=1}^{\infty }E_{n}\right )_{x} = \bigcup _{n=1}^{\infty }(E_{n})_{x}\), where \((E_{n})\) is a sequence of subsets of \(S_{1} \times S_{2}\).

  • 6.2 If \(m_{1}\) and \(m_{2}\) are \(\sigma \)-finite measures, show that the product measure \(m_{1} \times m_{2}\) is also \(\sigma \)-finite.

  • 6.3 If \(A \in \Sigma _{1}\) and \(B \in \Sigma _{2}\), prove that \((m_{1} \times m_{2})(A \times B) = m_{1}(A)m_{2}(B).\)

  • 6.4 A product set \(A_{1} \times A_{2}\) is said to be finite if \(m_{i}(A_{i}) < \infty \) for \(i= 1,2\). Show that product measure \(m_{1} \times m_{2}\) is the unique measure \(\mu \) on \((S_{1} \times S_{2}, \Sigma _{1} \otimes \Sigma _{2})\) for which

    \[ \mu (A_{1} \times A_{2}) = m_{1}(A_{1}) m_{2}(A_{2}),\]

    for all finite product sets.

    Hint: Use Dynkinâ€™s \(\pi - \lambda \) lemma.

  • 6.5

    • (a) Let \(f:S_{1} \rightarrow \R \) and \(g:S_{2} \rightarrow \R \) be measurable functions. Define \(h: S_{1} \times S_{2} \rightarrow \R \) by

      \[ h(x, y) = f(x) g(y),\]

      for all \(x \in S_{1}, y \in S_{2}\). Show that \(h\) is measurable.

    • (b) If \(f\) and \(g\) are integrable, show that \(h \) is also integrable and that

      \[ \int _{S_{1} \times S_{2}}h~d(m_{1} \times m_{2}) = \left (\int _{S_{1}}fdm_{1}\right )\left (\int _{S_{2}}gdm_{2}\right ).\]

  • 6.6 Let \(a_{ij} \geq 0 \) for all \(1 \leq i,j \leq \infty \). Show that

    \[ \sum _{i=1}^{\infty }\sum _{j=1}^{\infty }a_{ij} = \sum _{j=1}^{\infty }\sum _{i=1}^{\infty }a_{ij},\]

    in the sense that both double series converge to the same limit, or diverge together.

  • 6.7 Let \((S, \Sigma , m)\) be a \(\sigma \)-finite measure space and \(f:S \rightarrow \R \) be a non-negative measurable function. Define \(A_{f} = \{(x, t) \in S \times \R ; 0 \leq t \leq f(x)\}\). Show that \(A_{f} \in \Sigma \otimes {\cal B}(\R )\) and that

    \[ (m \times \lambda )(A_{f}) = \int _{S}f dm. \]

  • 6.8 Use Fubiniâ€™s theorem to prove that

    \[ \lim _{T \rightarrow \infty } \int _{0}^{T} \ds \frac {\sin (x)}{x}dx = \ds \frac {\pi }{2}.\]

    Hint: Write \(\frac {1}{x} = \int _{0}^{\infty }e^{-xy}dy.\)

  • 6.9

    • (a) Show that for the function \(f: \R ^{2} \rightarrow \R \), defined by

      \(f(x,y) = \ds \frac {xy}{(x^{2} + y^{2})^{2}}\), the iterated integrals \(\int _{-1}^{1}\left (\int _{-1}^{1}f(x,y)dy\right )dx\) and \(\int _{-1}^{1}\left (\int _{-1}^{1}f(x,y)dx\right )dy\) exist and are equal.

    • (b) Show that \(f\) is not integrable over the square \(-1 \leq x \leq 1\) and \(-1 \leq y \leq 1\).

  • 6.10 \((\star )\) This problem carries on from Problems 3.18-3.22. Like those problems, it deals with properties of the Fourier transform.

    Assume that \(f\) and \(g\) are integrable functions on \(\R \), and that \(g\) is bounded. Define the convolution \(f * g\) of \(f\) with \(g\) by

    \[ (f * g)(x) = \int _{\R }f(x - y)g(y)dy,\]

    for all \(x \in \R \). Show that \(|(f*g)(x)| < \infty \), and so \(f *g\) is a wellâ€“defined function from \(\R \) to \(\R \). Show further that \(f * g\) is both measurable and integrable, and that the Fourier transform of the convolution is the product of the Fourier transforms, i.e. that for all \(y \in \R \),

    \[ \widehat {f * g}(y) = \widehat {f}(y)\widehat {g}(y).\]
