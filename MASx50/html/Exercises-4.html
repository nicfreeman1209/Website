4.5 Exercises

  • 4.1 Use Theorem 4.1.1 to prove part 6 of Theorem 4.2.1.

  • 4.2 Write down â€˜probabilisticâ€™ versions of the monotone convergence theorem, Fatouâ€™s lemma and the dominated convergence theorem. That is, use random variables in place of measurable functions and expectation in place of the integral.

  • 4.3 Let \(X\) be a random variable and \(a \in \R \). Prove that

    \[ \E [\max \{X, a\}] \geq \max \{\E [X], a\}.\]

    Hint: Write \(\E [\max \{X, a\}]\) as an integral.

  • 4.4 Let \(U\) be a random variable such that \(\P [U=-1]=\P [U=1]=\frac 12\) and let \(V\) be a random variable such that \(\P [V=0]=\P [V=1]=\frac 12\), independent of \(U\). Let \(X=UV\) and \(Y=U(1-V)\). Show that \(\E [XY]=\E [X]\E [Y]\) but that \(X\) and \(Y\) are not independent.

  • 4.5

    • (a) Let \((A_n)\) be a sequence of independent events. Show that

      \(\seteqnumber{0}{4.}{0}\)

      \begin{equation} \label {eq:inf_indep} \P \l [\bigcap _{n\in \N } A_n\r ]=\prod _{n=1}^\infty \P [A_n]. \end{equation}

    • (b) Recall that we define independence of a sequence of events \((A_{n})\) in terms of finite subsequences (e.g. as in Section 4.4). An â€˜obviousâ€™ alternative definition might to be use (4.1) instead. Why is this not a sensible idea?

  • 4.6

    • (a) Let \(A\) and \(B\) be independent events. Show that their complements \(A^c\) and \(B^c\) are also independent.

    • (b) Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be independent random variables and \(f,g:\R \rightarrow \R \) be Borel measurable. Deduce that \(f(X)\) and \(g(Y)\) are also independent.

  • 4.7

    • (a) Let \(X\) be a random variable that takes values in \(\N \cup \{0\}\). Explain why \(X = \sum _{i=1}^{\infty }{\1}_{\{X \geq i\}}\) and hence show that

      \[\E [X] = \sum _{i=1}^{\infty }\P [X \geq i].\]

    • (b) Let \(Y\) be a non-negative random variable. Deduce that

      \[ \sum _{k=1}^{\infty }\P [Y \geq k] \;\leq \; \E [Y] \;\leq \; 1 + \sum _{k=1}^{\infty }\P [Y \geq k].\]

  • 4.8 Let \(X\) be a real-valued random variable defined on a probability space \((\Omega , {\cal F}, P)\). Use the result of Exercise 3.5 to derive the probabilistic version of Chebychevâ€™s inequality:

    \[ \P \l [|X - \mu | \geq c\r ] \leq \ds \frac {\var (X)}{c^{2}},\]

    where \(\var (X)\) and \(\E [X] = \mu \) are both assumed to be finite.

  • 4.9

    • (a) Suppose that \(X\) and \(Y\) are random variables and both \(X^{2}\) and \(Y^{2}\) are integrable. Prove the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality:

      \[ |\E [XY]| \leq (\E [X^{2}]^{\frac {1}{2}})(\E [Y^{2}]^{\frac {1}{2}}).\]

      Hint: Consider \(g(t) = \E [(X + tY)^{2}]\) as a quadratic function of \(t \in \R \).

    • (b) Deduce that if \(X^{2}\) is integrable then so is \(X\), and in fact \(|\E [X]|^{2} \leq \E [X^{2}]\).

    • (c) Let \(X\) be any random variable with a finite mean \(\E [X]=\mu \). Show that \(\E [X^{2}] < \infty \) if and only if \(\var (X) < \infty \).

  • 4.10

    • (a) Let \(X\) be a non-negative random variable and \(a > 0\). Show that \(\E (e^{-aX} ) \leq 1\).

    • (b) A random variable is said to have an exponential moment if \(\E (e^{a|X}) < \infty \) for some \(a > 0\). Show that \(X \sim N(0,1)\) has exponential moments for all \(a > 0\).

    • (c) If \(X\) has an exponential moment, deduce that it has moments to all orders, i.e. that \(\E (|X|^{n}) < \infty \) for all \(\nN \).

  • 4.11 Let \(X\) be a real-valued random variable with law \(p_{X}\) defined on a probability space \((\Omega , {\cal F}, \P )\). Show that for all bounded measurable functions \(f:\R \rightarrow \R \),

    \[ \int _{\Omega }f(X(\omega ))d\P (\omega ) = \int _{\R }f(x)dp_{X}(x).\]

    What can you say about these integrals when \(f\) is non-negative but not necessarily bounded?

    Hint: Begin with \(f\) an indicator function, then extend to simple, bounded non-negative and general bounded measurable functions.
